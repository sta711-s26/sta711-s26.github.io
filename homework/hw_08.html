Logistic regression power analysis

Suppose we are working with researchers interested in the relation between caffeine intake and insomnia. The researchers conduct a sleep study with a set of \(n\) subjects, all of whom have reported difficulty sleeping.

In the study, subjects are given a warm cup of coffee at 10pm, and then asked to go to bed directly after drinking the coffee. The subjects receive coffees containing different quantities of caffeine, with \(n/5\) patients randomly assigned to each of 5 treatment groups: 0mg caffeine, 25mg, 50mg, 75mg, and 100mg caffeine (for reference, a normal cup of coffee contains about 100mg of caffeine).

For each subject, the researchers record whether they fell asleep in the first hour after consuming the coffee (note: time-to-event analysis is probably better here, but that is outside the scope of this course). We plan to fit the following logistic regression model: \[Sleep_i|Caffeine_i \sim Bernoulli(p_i)\] \[\log \left( \frac{p_i}{1-p_i} \right) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 Caffeine_i\] where \(Sleep_i = 1\) if subject \(i\) fell asleep during the first hour, and \(Caffeine_i\) is the quantity of caffeine consumed (in mg) by subject \(i\).

To test for a relationship, the researchers plan to test \(H_0: \beta_1 = 0\) vs. \(H_A: \beta_1 \neq 0\) using a Wald test, rejecting if the p-value is \(< 0.05\).

Test statistic

From our asymptotic results for maximum likelihood estimators, we know that if our model is correctly specified, then

\[\widehat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \approx N(\boldsymbol{\beta}, \mathcal{I}_n^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\beta}))\] which means that

\[\widehat{\beta}_1 \approx N(\beta_1, [\mathcal{I}_n^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\beta})]_{2,2})\]

where \([\mathcal{I}_n^{-1}(\beta)]_{2,2}\) denotes the \([2,2]\) entry of the inverse Fisher information. We have also previously shown that

\[\mathcal{I}_n(\boldsymbol{\beta}) \approx \mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{V} \mathbf{X}\] with \(\mathbf{X}\) the design matrix and \(\mathbf{V} = \text{diag}(p_i(1 - p_i))\). So,

\[\widehat{\beta}_1 \approx N(\beta_1, [(\mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{V} \mathbf{X})^{-1}]_{2,2})\]

To test the hypotheses \(H_0: \beta_1 = 0\) vs. \(H_A: \beta_1 \neq 0\), the Wald test statistic is then

\[W = \dfrac{(\widehat{\beta}_1 - 0)^2}{[(\mathbf{X}^T \widehat{\mathbf{V}} \mathbf{X})^{-1}]_{2,2}}\]

where \(\widehat{\mathbf{V}} = \text{diag}(\widehat{p}_i(1 - \widehat{p}_i))\).

Power analysis

NoteQuestion 1
  1. Argue that, if \(H_0\) is true, then \(W \approx \chi^2_1\). We will reject when \(W > c\); find a rejection threshold \(c\), as the quantile of some distribution, such that our test is approximately size \(\alpha = 0.05\).

Now we want to analyze the power of the test when \(H_0\) is false. To do this, we need to know the distribution of the test statistic \(W\) under the alternative hypothesis. It turns out the appropriate distribution is the noncentral chi-squared distribution.

Definition (noncentral chi-squared): Let \(X_1,...,X_k \overset{indep.}{\sim} N(\mu_i, 1)\). Then, \[\sum \limits_{i=1}^k X_i^2 \sim \chi^2_k(\lambda),\] the noncentral chi-squared distribution with noncentrality parameter \(\lambda = \sum \limits_{i=1}^k \mu_i^2\).

Note that the usual \(\chi^2_k\) distribution is a special case of the noncentral chi-squared: \(\chi^2_k = \chi^2_k(0)\) (noncentral chi-squared with noncentrality parameter 0).

In R, probabilities for the \(\chi^2_k(\lambda)\) can be computed using the pchisq(...) function, which allows you to specify the noncentrality parameter (ncp).

\(t\) tests

Suppose we have data \(X_1,...,X_n\) iid from some population with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\). A Wald test for the population mean \(\mu\) would use the fact that

\[\dfrac{\sqrt{n}(\overline{X}_n - \mu)}{s} \approx N(0, 1)\]

where \(s^2 = \frac{1}{n-1} \sum \limits_{i=1}^n (X_i - \overline{X}_n)^2\). This approximate normality is the result of a combination of the CLT and the consistency of \(s^2\) as an estimator of \(\sigma^2\).

However, in other statistics courses, you have likely used a \(t\) distribution instead of a normal distribution to test these hypotheses! That is,

\[\dfrac{\sqrt{n}(\overline{X}_n - \mu)}{s} \approx t_{n-1},\]

where \(t_{n-1}\) is the \(t\) distribution with \(n-1\) degrees of freedom. The purpose of this part of the assignment is to justify the use of a \(t\) distribution in these situations.

The \(t\) distribution

When we want to show that a statistic follows a \(t\) distribution, it is helpful to think about where the \(t\) distribution comes from.

Definition: Let \(Z \sim N(0, 1)\) and \(V \sim \chi^2_d\) be independent. Then \[T = \frac{Z}{\sqrt{V/d}} \sim t_d\]

The sample mean

Suppose that we have \(X_1,...,X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} N(\mu, \sigma^2)\). Then,

\[\dfrac{\sqrt{n}(\overline{X}_n - \mu)}{s} = \dfrac{\sqrt{n}(\overline{X}_n - \mu)/\sigma}{s/\sigma} = \dfrac{\sqrt{n}(\overline{X}_n - \mu)/\sigma}{\sqrt{\frac{(n-1)s^2/\sigma^2}{(n-1)}}}\]

Let \(Z = \sqrt{n}(\overline{X}_n - \mu)/\sigma\) and \(V = (n-1)s^2/\sigma^2\). Then,

\[T = \dfrac{\sqrt{n}(\overline{X}_n - \mu)}{s} = \dfrac{Z}{\sqrt{V/(n-1)}}\]

Since \(X_1,...,X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} N(\mu, \sigma^2)\), then \(Z \sim N(0, 1)\). To show that \(T \sim t_{n-1}\), it just remains to show that

  • \(V \sim \chi^2_{n-1}\)
  • \(Z\) and \(V\) are independent

Fortunately, we can use a very cool result called Cochran’s theorem.

Cochran’s theorem

Theorem: Let \(Z_1,...,Z_n \overset{iid}{\sim} N(0, 1)\), and let \(\mathbf{z} = (Z_1,...,Z_n)^T\). Let \(\mathbf{A}_1,...,\mathbf{A}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) be symmetric matrices such that \(\mathbf{z}^T\mathbf{z} = \sum \limits_{i=1}^k \mathbf{z}^T \mathbf{A}_i \mathbf{z}\), and let \(r_i = rank(\mathbf{A}_i)\). Then the following are equivalent:

  • \(r_1 + \cdots + r_k = n\)
  • The \(\mathbf{z}^T \mathbf{A}_i \mathbf{z}\) are independent
  • Each \(\mathbf{z}^T \mathbf{A}_i \mathbf{z} \sim \chi^2_{r_i}\)

Putting it all together

Here we will put all of this information together to show that, indeed, \(T \sim t_{n-1}\).

UMP tests

The Neyman-Pearson lemma says that the most powerful size-\(\alpha\) test of the simple hypotheses \(H_0: \theta = \theta_0\) vs. \(H_A: \theta = \theta_1\) is the likelihood ratio test which rejects when \[\frac{L(\theta_1 | \mathbf{X})}{L(\theta_0 | \mathbf{X})} > k,\] where \(k\) is chosen so that the test is size \(\alpha\).

Using a likelihood ratio to test hypotheses is very common, but we often don’t have just a simple null and a simple alternative, so we don’t know if the likelihood ratio test is uniformly most powerful. However, there are certain situations in which a likelihood ratio test is still uniformly most powerful for composite hypotheses.

Monotone Likelihood Ratios

Definition (MLR): Let \(\mathcal{P} = \{f(x|\theta) : \theta \in \Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}\}\) be a family of pdfs or pmfs. We say that \(\mathcal{P}\) has monotone likelihood ratios (MLR) in the statistic \(T(X)\) if \(\dfrac{f(x|\theta_2)}{f(x|\theta_1)}\) is a non-decreasing function of \(T(x)\), for any pair \(\theta_2 > \theta_1\).

Theorem: Let \(\mathcal{P}\) be a family of distributions, and suppose we want to test \(H_0: \theta \leq \theta_0\) vs. \(H_A: \theta > \theta_0\) for some \(\theta_0 \in \mathbb{R}\). If \(\mathcal{P}\) has MLR in \(T(X)\), then the test that rejects \(H_0\) when \(T(X) > c_0\) is UMP at level \(\alpha = P_{\theta_0}(T(X) > c_0)\).

Corollary: Let \(\mathcal{P}\) be a family of distributions, and suppose we want to test \(H_0: \theta \geq \theta_0\) vs. \(H_A: \theta < \theta_0\) for some \(\theta_0 \in \mathbb{R}\) If \(\mathcal{P}\) has MLR in \(T(X)\), then the test that rejects \(H_0\) when \(T(X) < c_0\) is UMP at level \(\alpha = P_{\theta_0}(T(X) < c_0)\).
