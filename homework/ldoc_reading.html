LDOC reading

For the last day of class, I would like to have a discussion about the use (and mis-use) of hypothesis testing in the sciences, and implications for false positives and the reproducibility crisis.

To prepare for that discussion, I would like you to read a couple of articles, described below. These articles are pretty short and accessible.

“Why most published research findings are false”

In 2005, John Ioannidis published an influential paper titled “Why most published research findings are false”. In this article, he argues that, due to a variety of factors, most published research findings are in fact false discoveries.

Read through the article, and think about the following questions as you read:

  • How does Ioannidis come to the conclusion that most published research findings are false?
  • Which factors does Ioannidis claim will cause published research to be more likely to be false?
  • Do you think that most published research findings are false?

Brian Wansink, p-hacking and HARKing, and reproducibility

Brian Wansink was a famous professor and the head of the Food and Brand Lab at Cornell University. He published articles with catchy conclusions; for example, that people eat more when they are watching an action movie.

In 2017, Wansink became the center of a scientific ethics and reproducibility controversy, after he published a blog post which alluded to questionable use of data analysis and hypothesis testing in his studies.

Wansink’s apparent research practices raised concnerns about data management (independent investigators discovered a litany of data errors in some of his manuscripts), p-hacking (testing different hypotheses until you get a “significant” p-value), and hypothesizing after the results are known (i.e. HARKing, in which you choose your research question based on the data, rather than collecting data to answer a specific research question). All of these mis-uses of data and statistics are connected with a reproducibility crisis in several different fields.

Read this NPR article summarizing the Wansink story, and think about the following questions:

  • What issues were identified in Wansink’s work?
  • How could these issues result in a higher rate of false positives in his published research?

Optional: trying to estimate the actual proportion of false discoveries

John Ioannidis’ claim that “most published research findings are false” is disturbing. Can we try to estimate the actual proportion of false discoveries in the published literature?

Jager and Leek take this approach, and estimate that about 14% of published findings are false. Read the linked paper; how do the authors estimate the true proportion of false discoveries?
